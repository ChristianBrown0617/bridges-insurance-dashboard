Insurance Protection Overview

This dashboard highlights the essential protections most families should consider. Use it as a reference to help clients make informed decisions about protecting their finances without overspending.

Essential Coverage

  • Health Insurance: Protection from medical debt.
  • Auto Insurance: Legally required if you drive; shields from liability.
  • Home/Renters: Protects belongings, home, and liability.

Important Coverage

  • Life Insurance: Replaces income for dependents.
  • Disability Insurance: Protects your paycheck if you can't work.

Optional (After Basics)

  • Umbrella: Extra liability protection.
  • Flood / specialty: For specific risks where needed.

Insurance Basics: Key Terms

Understanding these terms makes everything else less confusing. Use the examples to explain trade-offs in real numbers.

Premium

The amount you pay (usually monthly) to keep coverage active. You pay this whether or not you file a claim.
Lower premium → usually higher costs if something happens.

Deductible

The amount you pay out of pocket on a covered claim before insurance pays.
Example: $1,000 deductible → you pay first $1,000 of a covered loss.

Co-pay

A fixed amount you pay for certain services (e.g., $20 doctor visit). Insurance pays the rest.

Coinsurance

You pay a percentage of costs after the deductible; the plan pays the rest.
Example: 20% coinsurance → you pay 20%, plan pays 80%.

Out-of-Pocket Maximum

The most you’ll pay in a year for covered services. After you hit this limit, the plan pays 100% of covered costs.

Claim

A request to your insurer to pay for a covered loss or service.

Health Insurance

Health coverage protects against medical bills that can quickly become unmanageable. For most families, this is the highest priority.

What It Is & Where to Get It

  • Employer plans
  • Healthcare.gov / ACA Marketplace (with subsidies for lower incomes)
  • Medicaid / CHIP for qualifying individuals & families
  • Medicare for 65+ or certain disabilities
Goal: avoid catastrophic medical debt.

Keeping It Affordable

  • Check Medicaid / subsidy eligibility.
  • Compare plans on total cost: premium + deductible + out-of-pocket max.
  • Use in-network providers whenever possible.
  • Higher deductibles can be okay if you have an emergency fund.

Auto Insurance

Required if you drive. Protects you from lawsuits, medical costs, and damage.

Coverage Types

  • Liability (Required): Injuries & damage you cause.
  • Collision: Your car in a crash.
  • Comprehensive: Theft, fire, weather, etc.
  • Uninsured/Underinsured: When the other driver has too little or no coverage.

Making It Affordable

  • Maintain a clean driving record.
  • Bundle with home/renters for discounts.
  • Raise deductibles if you can handle the risk.
  • Ask about all discounts (safe driver, student, multi-car).
Never drive uninsured.

Homeowners & Renters Insurance

Protects your home or belongings plus liability if someone is hurt on your property.

Homeowners

  • Dwelling (structure) coverage at replacement cost.
  • Personal property (your stuff).
  • Liability for injuries on your property.
  • Loss of use (hotel, etc. if home uninhabitable).
Often required by mortgage lenders.

Renters

  • Personal property coverage (furniture, electronics, clothes).
  • Liability if you accidentally damage the unit or someone is hurt.
  • Very low cost (often $10–$20/month).
Landlord’s policy does NOT cover your stuff.

Life Insurance

If someone depends on your income, life insurance helps support them if you die. Term life is usually the simplest and most affordable option.

Types & Amount

  • Term life: 10–30 years; good for most families.
  • Rule of thumb: ~5–10× annual income (adjust to debts & kids).
  • Many employer policies are small; consider adding your own.

Who Needs It

  • Parents with kids or anyone with dependents.
  • Partners who rely on shared income.
  • Less urgent for single adults with no dependents.
Focus on affordability, not fancy add-ons.

Disability Insurance

Protects your paycheck if an illness or injury stops you from working. Statistically more likely to be used than life insurance.

What It Is

  • Short-term and long-term disability (often through employers).
  • Replaces a portion of income (commonly 50–70%).

Where to Start

  • Check employer benefits first.
  • If not available, explore individual coverage if affordable.
Key safety net for households living paycheck to paycheck.

Umbrella Insurance

Extra liability coverage that sits on top of auto and home policies. Often inexpensive; useful once you have assets or higher risk factors.

How It Works

  • Kicks in after auto/home liability limits are used up.
  • Common starting limit: $1 million.

Who Should Consider It

  • Homeowners and higher earners.
  • Households with teen drivers, pools, rentals, or other risk.
Consider after essentials are in place.

Tips to Save on Insurance Costs

Help clients lower costs without cutting the protections that matter.

Smart Ways to Save

  • Bundle auto + home/renters.
  • Increase deductibles (with an emergency fund).
  • Shop quotes periodically, especially at renewal.
  • Ask for discounts (safe driver, student, security systems).

Other Strategies

  • Use pre-tax accounts (HSA/FSA) for medical costs where allowed.
  • Maintain good credit (where allowed by law).
  • Pay annually instead of monthly if discounts are offered.
  • Review coverage yearly so it fits your current life.
Complete Insurance Action Plan

Use this checklist to confirm key protections and avoid common gaps. Check off items as they’re completed.

Essential Coverage (Must-Have)

Have Health Insurance
Each family member should have basic health coverage (employer, ACA Marketplace, Medicaid/Medicare, etc.).
Medical bills are a leading cause of financial stress — don’t skip this step.
Carry Auto Insurance (if you drive)
  • Keep at least state-required liability at all times; consider higher limits.
  • Decide if collision/comprehensive are needed (newer or valuable vehicles).
  • List all household drivers on the policy.
Never drive uninsured.
Protect Your Home or Belongings
  • If you own a home: maintain homeowners insurance with dwelling + liability.
  • If you rent: carry renters insurance for belongings + liability.
Don’t assume your landlord’s policy covers your stuff.

Important Coverage (If You Have Dependents)

Life Insurance for Family’s Breadwinners
  • Ensure term life (or similar) is in place if others rely on your income.
  • Consider ~5–10× income; adjust to debts and family needs.
  • Review beneficiaries and coverage as life changes.
Disability Insurance (Income Protection)
  • Check employer benefits for short-/long-term disability.
  • If not offered and affordable, explore individual coverage.
You’re more likely to face a disability than an early death — this is a key safety net.

Optional Coverage (After Basics)

Umbrella Insurance (if appropriate)
Consider if you have savings, a home, teen drivers, or higher lawsuit risk. Often inexpensive for $1M+ extra liability.

Financial Preparation

Emergency Fund for Deductibles
Aim for at least enough savings to cover health/auto/home deductibles (e.g., $1,000–$2,000+).
Use Discounts & Subsidies
Take advantage of available discounts (multi-policy, safe driver, etc.) and any health insurance subsidies or employer contributions.

Ongoing Management

Review Policies Yearly
Check coverage amounts, beneficiaries, and premiums whenever life changes or at renewal.
Know How to File a Claim
Keep policy numbers and contact info handy; know which number or app to use.
Avoid Lapses
Use reminders or auto-pay so coverage doesn’t accidentally cancel.
Ask Questions
Encourage clients to ask when they’re unsure. Informed choices beat guessing.
